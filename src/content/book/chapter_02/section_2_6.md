# 2.6  Summary

Chapter 2 was the running-code chapter. You stood up an OpenVLA-7B checkpoint (Kim et al., 2024, arXiv:2406.09246), pointed it at a LIBERO tabletop scene (Liu et al., 2023, arXiv:2306.03310), watched it succeed on the task it was trained for, and watched it fail in three specific ways once you removed something it quietly depended on. That's the artifact the rest of the book keeps pointing back to. This summary collects the load-bearing ideas in one place. It's shorter than §1.6 because the chapter was shorter, but structured the same way so you can use it as a checklist before turning the page.

## The four ideas worth carrying forward

A VLA is six boxes, not one black box. §2.1 named the four commitments (how images become tokens, how the head emits actions, what dataset taught the model physics, what evaluation discipline gets enforced). §2.3 exposed the loop those commitments live inside: a visual encoder, a language tokenizer, a transformer trunk, an action head emitting discrete tokens, a detokenizer mapping tokens to a 7-vector, and a simulator closing the loop with a fresh image. Six boxes, each one a component you can swap, debug, or replace independently. The temptation when reading a new VLA paper is to treat the model as an undifferentiated blob of parameters. The discipline this chapter pushed on you instead is reading the paper as a set of choices for each of those six boxes. When you read that π0 (Black et al., 2024, arXiv:2410.24164) "uses flow matching for action," you should now be able to locate that sentence at box four, the action head, and ask what it implies for box five (the detokenizer becomes a flow-matching sampler) and box three (the training loss changes from cross-entropy to a flow-matching objective). That re-reading is the whole point of having stood up the loop yourself.

Action tokenization is the architectural trick that made the rest possible. §2.1 and §2.3 circled back to this idea three times because it keeps mattering. The seven integers OpenVLA emits per step aren't text tokens reused at inference; they're integer IDs in the bottom 256 entries of the Llama-2 vocabulary, repurposed during training to mean "discretized action bin 0 through 255." Cross-entropy loss on those tokens is the entire training objective. The architectural simplicity that follows, same transformer, same optimizer, same training infrastructure as a language model, is the single biggest reason a 7-billion-parameter VLA was a tractable engineering project at all. The cost is discreteness, and it shows up in §2.4 as the third silent failure: a wrong `unnorm_key` produces seven perfectly plausible integers that decode to a wildly off-scale 7-vector. Chapters 10 and 13 take the discrete-versus-continuous trade-off apart in detail. What you should carry forward from Chapter 2 is simply that the trade-off exists, that OpenVLA sits on one side of it, and that the model running on your machine right now is a discrete-action VLA whose smoothness ceiling is set by bin width.

Running and working are not the same loop. §2.4 is the chapter's clearest payoff. A loop completing four hundred steps without raising an exception isn't a loop that solves the task, and the gap between the two is exactly where silent failures live. The three you saw, a flipped image (LIBERO returns the agent-view buffer upside down), a wrong prompt template (OpenVLA's training-time format isn't the obvious one), a wrong `unnorm_key` (per-embodiment normalization statistics are how the detokenizer recovers physical units), aren't a complete list. They're a kind of bug. Each one has the same shape: a piece of metadata that was implicit in the training data and that you have to make explicit at inference time, with nothing raised if you get it wrong. The general lesson, one §17.4 returns to, is that the most expensive bugs in a deployed VLA are the ones that don't crash. Chapter 2 gave you three reproducible examples on a laptop; Chapter 17 generalizes the diagnosis discipline to hardware. The chapters in between are largely about the kinds of metadata, embodiment, camera pose, action normalization, prompt template, that have to travel with a model from training to inference.

Evaluation must outlast the cherry-picked clip. §2.4 closed with the point that a single rollout tells you almost nothing. Twenty rollouts on twenty logged seeds, across three initial conditions, with one camera variation, is the minimum unit of evidence that lets you compare two versions of the same model honestly. The version of that discipline you ran in §2.4 was small; the version Chapter 15 develops scales to real-robot evaluation, where variance runs higher and each trial costs minutes rather than seconds. The skill worth internalizing here is logging the seed and the initial condition before you watch the rollout, not after. The temptation to publish the good clip is a permanent feature of this field. The only protection against fooling yourself is the rigor of the logs you actually keep.

## What you should be able to do now

Four concrete things, in increasing order of how much they matter for the rest of the book.

You should be able to re-run a single LIBERO rollout from a logged seed and reproduce the trajectory step for step. This is the smallest unit of the discipline §2.4 introduced. Can't reproduce your own rollout? Then you can't debug it, and if you can't debug it, you can't improve the policy. The recipe is the one from §2.3: pin the simulator seed, pin the initial condition file, pin the model dtype, and log the action vector at every step. When you flip to Chapter 16 to fine-tune on your own data, this same reproducibility hygiene becomes the difference between a fine-tune that converges and one you simply can't diagnose.

You should be able to read a new VLA inference script and place each line in one of the six boxes from §2.3. Concretely: hit a fresh policy on Hugging Face, say an OpenVLA fine-tune for a new embodiment or a community port of Octo (arXiv:2405.12213), and you should be able to skim its `predict_action` (or equivalent) call and identify which line tokenizes the image, which tokenizes the prompt, which runs the transformer trunk, which samples the action, and which maps that sample back to physical units. This six-box reading skill is what the rest of Part 4 assumes you already have. Chapter 11 formalizes it; Chapter 2 gave you the practice reps.

You should be able to predict which of the three silent failures from §2.4 a given bug is, from its symptom alone. Robot moves in slow random scribbles: wrong prompt template, the model isn't in action-decoding mode. Robot moves decisively in the wrong direction: flipped image, the policy is acting on a world that doesn't exist. Robot moves at the wrong scale, way too small or way too large: wrong `unnorm_key`, the detokenizer is applying the wrong embodiment's statistics. None of these symptoms throws an exception. The mapping from symptom to cause is the muscle separating someone who's deployed a VLA before from someone who hasn't, and Chapter 2 gave you the three reps needed to start building it.

You should be able to use the §2.5 map to plan your own reading path. Short on time? The minimum path runs Chapter 3 (only the parts you don't already know), Chapter 6 (imitation learning), Chapter 8 (transformers), Chapter 11 (CLIP to BC-Z to RT-1, arXiv:2212.06817), Chapter 12 (the scaling moment), and Chapter 16 (fine-tuning). Project-driven and want the canonical reference for one specific model instead? Appendix F (the VLA model zoo) and Appendix E.2 (the chapter-by-chapter reading list) cross-reference everything. The book is designed to be read non-linearly after Chapter 2, and the map is what lets you do that without losing your place.

## Where the chapter has set up the rest of the book

Three forward references are worth naming again, since they structure what comes next. The six-box decomposition from §2.3 is the spine of every model walkthrough in Part 4 (Chapters 11 through 14): each chapter re-enters the loop and replaces one or two boxes. The three silent failures from §2.4 get revisited in §17.2 (runtime monitoring as the deployment-time response to the same class of bug) and §15.4 (real-robot evaluation, where higher variance makes silent failures hardest to catch). The four commitments from §2.1, image encoding, action head, training data, evaluation discipline, are the four axes Chapter 12 uses to compare RT-2 (arXiv:2307.15818), OpenVLA, and Octo head-to-head, and the same four axes Chapter 13 uses to argue for π0's design.

The chapter hasn't covered three things you might have expected. It hasn't trained anything; you ran a pretrained checkpoint and modified no weights, and Chapter 16 is the training chapter. It hasn't derived any of the math behind the transformer or the cross-entropy loss; Chapters 3 and 8 handle that, in that order. And it hasn't touched a real robot at all, since everything you ran was in simulation. Chapter 17 covers the gap from simulation to deployment, and Chapter 15 covers the evaluation methodology that has to bridge it. These omissions are deliberate. The point of Chapter 2 was keeping the first VLA you ran small enough to hold in your head, with the implicit promise that the rest of the book widens the aperture without throwing away the picture you've just formed.

§2.x closes the chapter with one hands-on exercise, a deliberate variation on the §2.3 script that reproduces one of the three silent failures on demand, and the full reading list for the chapter.
